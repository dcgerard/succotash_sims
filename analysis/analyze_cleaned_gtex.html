Initial GTEX Analysis

David Gerard

2016-05-26

Brief Summary

Read in the cleaned heart gtex data and estimate the number of hidden confounders with sva::num.sv().

library(ggplot2)
load(file = "../output/cleaned_gtex_data/heart.Rd")
k <- sva::num.sv(dat = heart$Y, mod = heart$X)
k
## [1] 14

\(Y\) is log(COUNTS + 1) of the top 10000 expressed genes. Samples from the same individual were averaged, resulting in \(n =\) 293. \(X\) contains just an intercept and an indicator for gender. The idea here is that genes on sex chromosomes are “positive controls,” so the best method will be that which has the most genes on sex chromosomes among the most significant genes. This is the setup that Gagnon-Bartsch and Speed (2012) and Wang et al. (2015) use to evaluate performance between these confounder adjustment methods.

Control genes are the housekeeping genes identified by Eisenberg and Levanon (2013). This is a different list than that used by Gagnon-Bartsch and Speed (2012), which was drawn from Eisenberg and Levanon (2003). In total, there are 3324 housekeeping genes among the 10000 most expressed genes in the GTEX heart data.

Analysis

Now fit RUVASH and RUV4.

onsex <- heart$chrom == "X" | heart$chrom == "Y"
ruvash_out <- ashr::ash_ruv(Y = t(heart$Y), X = heart$X, ctl = heart$ctl, k = k)
ruvash_onsex <- onsex[order(ruvash_out$lfdr)]
ruv4_out <- ruv::RUV4(Y = t(heart$Y), X = heart$X[, 2, drop = FALSE], ctl = heart$ctl, k = k)
ruv4_onsex <- onsex[order(ruv4_out$p)]
num_sig <- sum(ruvash_out$lfdr < 0.5)

RUVASH says that 33 genes have a posterior probability less than 0.5 of being zero. RUVASH says that 27 of the top 33 genes are on a sex chromosome while RUV4 says that 27 of the top 33 genes are on a sex chromosome. The ordering that RUV4 and RUVASH give to the genes is very similar for the very significant genes. We can calculate their Kendall’s tau.

lfdr_order <- order(ruvash_out$lfdr)
tau <- cor(c(ruv4_out$p)[lfdr_order[1:num_sig]], ruvash_out$lfdr[lfdr_order[1:num_sig]], method = "kendall")
tau
## [1] 0.9934

Their rankings actually only differ once in the first 33 genes. The ordering later on gets a little different.

qplot(rank(c(ruv4_out$p))[lfdr_order[1:50]], rank(ruvash_out$lfdr)[lfdr_order[1:50]],
      xlab = "RUV4 Rank", ylab = "RUVASH Rank", main = "Rankings",
      color = onsex[lfdr_order[1:50]]) +
    guides(color=guide_legend(title="On Sex?"))

Comparison to SUCCOTASH

Now run SUCCOTASH and see if we get anything very different.

succ_out <- succotashr::succotash(Y = t(heart$Y), X = heart$X, k = k)
## optmethod = "em" works a little better for normal likelihood,
## but here goes nothing!
succ_onsex <- onsex[order(succ_out$lfdr)]

SUCCOTASH says that 28 of the top 33 are on a sex chromosome. The ordering between SUCCOTASH and RUVASH is also very similar for the top few genes.

qplot(rank(c(succ_out$lfdr))[lfdr_order[1:50]], rank(ruvash_out$lfdr)[lfdr_order[1:50]],
      xlab = "SUCCOTASH Rank", ylab = "RUVASH Rank", main = "Rankings",
      color = onsex[lfdr_order[1:50]]) +
    guides(color=guide_legend(title="On Sex?"))

SUCCOTASH says that 31 of the genes have a posterior probability less than a half of being zero.

The estimate of \(\pi_0\) for SUCCOTASH is 0.9943 and that of RUVASH is 0.9941. When I run qvalue with the p-values from RUV4 I get

qout <- qvalue::qvalue(p = ruv4_out$p)
qout$pi0
## [1] 0.5104

Which is very different from the estimates of RUVASH and SUCCOTASH.

What About OLS + Limma?

ols_out <- limma::lmFit(object = heart$Y, design = heart$X)
lim_out <- limma::ebayes(fit = ols_out)
lim_p <- lim_out$p.value[, 2]
lim_onsex <- onsex[order(lim_p)]

Limma says that 21 of the top 33 are on a sex chromosome. The ordering of genes via Limma and RUVASH are very different after the first 20 genes.

qplot(rank(c(lim_p))[lfdr_order[1:50]], rank(ruvash_out$lfdr)[lfdr_order[1:50]],
